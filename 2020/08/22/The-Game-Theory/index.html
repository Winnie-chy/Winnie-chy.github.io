The Game Theory

In economics, there is a word everyone is familiar with called opportunity cost which means the best alternative forgone. Also, when I knew the concept of game theory in an economic book, I realized that in some ways, game theory was very similar to opportunity cost.

When producers make an economic decision, it certainly contain gains and losses. That’s why they have to make the optimal decision after careful consideration and estimation of advantages and disadvantages. Unlike opportunity cost, game theory is not about making a decision, but about two people using and changing their strategies in order to win in an equal game. Game theory considers the predictive behavior and actual behavior of individuals in the game and studies their optimization strategies.

In game theory, the most famous and easy-understood example is the ‘Prisoner’s Dilemma’ model given by Tucker. The model tells us the story of a policeman and a thief in a special way. Suppose two thieves conspire to commit a crime and are caught by the police.

The police put the two men in separate rooms for questioning. The policy was: If both suspects confessed and the evidence was conclusive, both were convicted, and each had to spend eight years in prison. If only one of the suspects confesses and the other denies the confession, the person who denied will be given an additional two years in prison, and the offender’s sentence will be reduced by eight years and will be released immediately. If both men deny it, the police can’t convict them on other charges for lack of evidence but can send each man to prison for one year. The following table shows the payment matrix for this game.

A\B confess deny
confess 8\8 0\10
deny 10\0 1\1

From the table, since neither person knows what the other will choose, confession is always optimal because it makes sure you’re not at a disadvantage between two people. But if they all choose to “deny”, they will each be sentenced to only one year in prison. As a result, (denial, denial) is Pareto optimal, because any other action choice combination that deviates from this action choice combination will make a person in a worse situation. However, “confession” is the dominant strategy of any criminal suspect, and (confession, confession) is a dominant strategic equilibrium, Nash equilibrium. It is not hard to see that there is a conflict between Nash equilibrium and Pareto here.

Of course, this example is just for a better understanding of game theory, and it doesn’t work in psychologically or socially ways. I don’t understand this complicated concept at first, but after seeing this example, I think game theory is two people making decisions without letting each other know. And that’s why there is more uncertainty than the opportunity cost.
